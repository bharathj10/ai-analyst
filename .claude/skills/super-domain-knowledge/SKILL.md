# Australian Superannuation Domain Knowledge

## System overview

Australia's superannuation system is a mandatory, privately managed DC (defined contribution) system with ~$3.9 trillion AUM (2024), covering ~16 million working Australians. It is regulated by APRA (prudential), ASIC (consumer/disclosure), and the ATO (tax/compliance).

Three phases:
- **Accumulation** — employer + voluntary contributions grow with earnings (15% tax on contributions and earnings)
- **Transition to Retirement (TTR)** — from preservation age, draw down while still working (limited)
- **Retirement / Pension phase** — Account Based Pension (ABP); earnings tax 0%, drawings tax-free after 60

## Regulatory framework

### Primary legislation
- **Superannuation Industry (Supervision) Act 1993 (SIS Act)** — the constitutional document; trustee duties, operating standards, preservation
- **Superannuation (Excess Concessional Contributions) Tax Act** — contribution excess penalties
- **Income Tax Assessment Act 1997** — tax concessions, SMSF rules

### APRA prudential standards (key ones)
| Standard | Subject |
|---|---|
| SPS 515 | Strategic planning and member outcomes |
| SPS 530 | Investment governance (asset allocation, liquidity, derivatives) |
| SPS 220 | Risk management |
| SPS 250 | Insurance in superannuation |
| SPS 310 | Audit requirements |
| SPS 114 | Operational risk financial requirement |

### ASIC key frameworks
- RG 274 — Product design and distribution obligations
- RG 97 — Disclosure of fees and costs (complex; differentiate between investment fees, administration fees, transaction costs)
- Superannuation Product Disclosure Statements (PDS)

### ATO key roles
- SMSF regulation (600,000+ SMSFs, ~$1M average balance)
- TFN withholding (applies at highest marginal rate + Medicare if no TFN)
- Lost member register; unclaimed superannuation money
- SuperStream (standardised contribution data standard)

## Contribution rules (FY2025-26)

### Superannuation Guarantee (SG)
| FY | SG Rate |
|---|---|
| FY2013 | 9.25% |
| FY2014–2021 | 9.5% (frozen) |
| FY2022 | 10.0% |
| FY2023 | 10.5% |
| FY2024 | 11.0% |
| FY2025 | 11.5% |
| FY2026+ | 12.0% (legislated cap) |

SG applies on Ordinary Time Earnings (OTE), not total package. Employer must contribute by 28th day after quarter end.

### Contribution caps (FY2025)
| Type | Cap | Tax treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Concessional (CC) | $30,000 | 15% contributions tax; deductible for self-employed |
| Non-concessional (NCC) | $120,000 | After-tax dollars; no additional tax |
| NCC bring-forward | Up to $360,000 over 3 years | Triggered if NCC > annual cap; requires TSB < $1.66M |
| CGT cap | $1,780,000 (lifetime) | Small business CGT concession proceeds |

Catch-up concessional contributions: unused CC cap (rolling 5 years) if TSB < $500k.

### Total Superannuation Balance (TSB) thresholds (FY2025)
| TSB | NCC eligibility |
|---|---|
| < $1.66M | Full NCC cap |
| $1.66M – $1.78M | $80,000 |
| $1.78M – $1.9M | $40,000 |
| ≥ $1.9M | Nil |

Transfer Balance Cap: $1.9M (FY2025) — lifetime limit on pension phase balances. Indexed.

## Tax framework

| Event | Tax rate |
|---|---|
| Employer SG contributions | 15% contributions tax |
| Salary sacrifice contributions | 15% contributions tax |
| Low-income earners (income < ~$37k) | LISTO offsets up to $500 |
| High-income earners (income > $250k) | Additional 15% Division 293 tax |
| Earnings (accumulation phase) | 15% (10% on long-term capital gains) |
| Earnings (pension phase) | 0% |
| Benefits — age 60+ | Tax-free (taxed source); tax-free (untaxed source has limits) |
| Benefits — age 57-59 (preserved age but < 60) | Low-rate threshold ($235k lifetime) then 15% + Medicare |
| Death benefits to tax dependants | Tax-free |
| Death benefits to non-dependants | 15-30% on taxed element |

## Preservation

Preserved until a **condition of release** is met:
- Attaining preservation age (60 for those born after 1 July 1964)
- Retirement (with intent not to return to work for those <60)
- Severe financial hardship (limited amounts)
- Compassionate grounds (ATO approval)
- Terminal medical condition
- Permanent incapacity
- Death

**Preservation age by birth year:**
| Born | Preservation age |
|---|---|
| Before 1 Jul 1960 | 55 (already reached) |
| 1 Jul 1960 – 30 Jun 1961 | 56 |
| 1 Jul 1961 – 30 Jun 1962 | 57 |
| 1 Jul 1962 – 30 Jun 1963 | 58 |
| 1 Jul 1963 – 30 Jun 1964 | 59 |
| From 1 Jul 1964 | 60 |

## Key performance metrics

### Fund-level KPIs
| Metric | Definition | Benchmark context |
|---|---|---|
| Net return after fees & tax | Primary performance metric | MySuper median ~8.5% p.a. (10-year to 2024) |
| Investment fee ratio | Fees/FUM | Industry fund median ~0.6-0.8%; retail ~0.9-1.2% |
| Administration fee | Flat $ + % above threshold | APRA performance test uses dollar fee benchmarks |
| Insurance premium | Member cost for death/TPD/IP cover | Varies by age, occupation, balance |
| Member satisfaction (NPS) | Net Promoter Score | Industry average ~+35; top funds ~+55 |
| FUM growth | New money in - outflows + earnings | Positive flow signal; driven by net new members + contribution growth |
| Contribution per member | Mean/median employer + voluntary | Signals salary level and engagement |
| Salary sacrifice participation rate | % of members making voluntary CC | Benchmark: ~20-25% for large industry funds |
| Account consolidation rate | Members holding multiple accounts | APRA pushing reduction; 30-40% have multiple accounts |
| Active engagement rate | Logged in / changed investment option | Low engagement is default risk indicator |
| Claim rate | Insurance claims per 1,000 members | Actuarial key metric; claims reserve adequacy |

### APRA Performance Test (Annual)
APRA compares each MySuper product's 8-year net return against a benchmark (strategic asset allocation × index returns). Products that underperform by >0.5% p.a. are required to notify members. Two consecutive failures = fund cannot accept new MySuper members.

Results published annually, usually August. Key data source: APRA Annual Fund-Level Superannuation Statistics (AFLSS).

## ASFA Retirement Standard (2024)
Target income for a "comfortable" retirement:

| Lifestyle | Single | Couple |
|---|---|---|
| Comfortable | $51,630 p.a. | $72,663 p.a. |
| Modest | $32,030 p.a. | $46,494 p.a. |

Assumes: own home outright, in good health, aged 65–84. Comfortable = ASFA's "adequate" standard for middle-class lifestyle.

**Replacement rate target:** 60-70% of pre-retirement income is the international standard; OECD benchmark is 58% (mandatory + voluntary net).

Australia's estimated system replacement rate: ~60-70% at median earnings — broadly adequate but bottom quartile earners reliant on Age Pension supplement.

**Age Pension:** Full pension ~$28,000 p.a. single. Assets test and income test mean most retirees with super will receive partial pension (means-tested). Full pension cut-off: ~$300k assets (homeowner, single).

## Retirement Income Covenant (2022)

SIS Act requirement for all trustees (from 1 July 2022) to formulate, review, and give effect to a **Retirement Income Strategy** for their members. Must cover:
1. Maximising expected retirement income
2. Managing risks to income (longevity, investment, inflation)
3. Flexible access to savings

Signals shift from accumulation focus to member outcome focus. Trustees now must think about decumulation, not just accumulation.

## Insurance within super

Three types:
- **Death (life) cover** — lump sum to beneficiaries
- **Total and Permanent Disability (TPD)** — lump sum if unable to work
- **Income Protection (IP)** — monthly benefit (up to 70-75% of income, 2-5 year benefit period common)

Default insurance: most MySuper products provide default death + TPD cover. IP less common as default.

Voluntary cancellation: since 2019 *Protecting Your Super* legislation, inactive accounts (<$6,000 or no contributions for 16 months) have insurance cancelled unless member opts in. Reduces insurance erosion of small balances.

Key analytical flags:
- Young members (<25) and low-balance members often have inappropriate default cover
- High-risk occupations (trades, healthcare) have higher claims — occupation-rating important
- Claims spike at ages 50-65 (TPD) and 35-55 (IP)

## Member lifecycle touchpoints

Key moments that drive engagement and product decisions:

1. **Joining** — default fund assignment (employer default or stapling), TFN lodgement, beneficiary nomination
2. **Job change** — account consolidation decision (stapling since Nov 2021 means fund follows member)
3. **Life events** — marriage, children, buying home (FHSS scheme), divorce (splitting orders)
4. **Career peak (40s-50s)** — highest contribution capacity, salary sacrifice decisions, investment option review
5. **Pre-retirement (55-65)** — TTR strategy, contribution catch-up, transition planning
6. **Retirement** — ABP commencement, drawdown strategy, Centrelink interaction
7. **Death** — binding/non-binding nominations, estate planning

**Stapling (from Nov 2021):** New employees are now "stapled" to their existing super fund rather than joining employer's default fund. Disrupts fund growth through new members; increases importance of retention.

### Netherlands (ABP/PFZW model) — often cited as "world's best"
- Near-universal mandatory coverage (~90% of workers)
- Collective DC (CDC) model: pooled risk between members
- ~190% of GDP in pension assets
- Multi-employer industry-wide funds (ABP = govt/education; PFZW = healthcare)
- Strong governance: independent boards, mandatory actuarial review
- Shifting from DB to CDC to reduce intergenerational cross-subsidy

### Denmark (ATP + labour market pensions)
- ATP: basic supplementary pension (~flat DKK contribution)
- Labour market pensions: industry-wide, negotiated by unions/employers (~12-17% of salary)
- Total contribution rate ~15-17%
- Longevity-linked: benefits adjusted for life expectancy changes

### Canada (CPP + workplace pensions)
- CPP (Canada Pension Plan): mandatory for employees; expanded in 2019 (CPP2)
- OTPP (Ontario Teachers), OMERS, CPPIB: world-class infrastructure investors; pioneered direct investing
- "Canadian Model" = large pension funds as direct infrastructure/PE investors (no manager fees at that layer)
- Australia's large funds (AustralianSuper, ART) increasingly mimicking Canadian model

### UK (NEST + master trusts)
- Auto-enrolment since 2012: employee default unless opt-out
- Minimum contribution: 8% total (3% employer, 5% employee) — lower than Australia
- NEST: government-backed default fund for small employers
- Move from DB to DC: 80% of private sector workers now DC
- Weakness: low default contribution rate → retirement adequacy gap

### US (401(k) + IRA)
- Voluntary, employer-matching culture
- 401(k): up to $23,500 (2025) pre-tax; employer matches vary widely (0-6%)
- IRA: individual, $7,000 limit
- No compulsion → ~45% of workers have no workplace retirement savings
- Defined benefit largely gone from private sector (only government/union DB remains)
- CalPERS/CalSTRS: major public-sector DB funds ($500B+); actuarially stressed in many states

### Singapore (CPF)
- Mandatory: employer (17%) + employee (20%) = 37% total for peak earners
- Multi-account: OA (housing), SA (retirement), MA (healthcare)
- Housing integration: can use OA to buy HDB flat — controversial; reduces retirement savings
- CPF LIFE: mandatory annuity from $60,000 SA balance
- Government-managed via GIC; investment returns stable but lower than Australia

### Chile (AFP) — cautionary tale
- Pioneered mandatory private DC in 1981
- Six private AFP managers; competition drove fees down but not enough
- Outcome: median replacement rate ~35-40%, below OECD standard
- Pandemic withdrawals (2020-21): allowed 10%+10%+10% withdrawals — depleted system
- Reform underway: adding 6% employer contribution, collective element
- Lesson: compulsion + DC alone insufficient without adequate rates + state supplement

### Sweden (Premiepension)
- 2.5% of salary directed to individual investment account (Premiepension)
- 16% goes to notional DB (NDC) pay-as-you-go component
- Default fund (AP7 Såfa): lifecycle glide path, globally diversified
- ~800 fund options; "too much choice" shown to reduce engagement
- AP buffer funds (AP1-4, AP6): ~$200B total; sophisticated long-term investors

## Common analytical frameworks in super

### Retirement adequacy analysis
1. Project balance to retirement age (compound interest model with contribution + earnings)
2. Convert to income stream (account-based pension, drawdown rate)
3. Compare to ASFA comfortable/modest standards and replacement rate target
4. Factor in Age Pension eligibility (means-tested top-up)
5. Sensitivity analysis on return assumptions (+/- 1%), contribution rate, retirement age

### Member segmentation
Common dimensions:
- **Balance quartile** (Q1 < $50k, Q2 $50-150k, Q3 $150-350k, Q4 > $350k)
- **Age band** (<30, 30-39, 40-49, 50-59, 60-64, 65+)
- **Contribution pattern** (SG only, salary sacrifice, voluntary non-concessional, nil)
- **Engagement level** (last login, investment option changes, insurance opt-in/out)
- **Life stage** (accumulation, pre-retirement, retirement)
- **Employer type** (large corporate, SME, casual/gig)

### Fund performance attribution
1. Strategic asset allocation (SAA) benchmark return
2. Tactical asset allocation (TAA) deviation
3. Manager selection alpha within each asset class
4. Currency impact (for international assets)
5. Fees and tax drag
Net return = SAA return + TAA ± manager alpha ± currency - fees - tax

### Fee analysis (RG 97 compliant)
- Investment fee (charged within investment option)
- Administration fee (flat $ + % of balance)
- Transaction costs (buy-sell spread, brokerage)
- Performance fee (if applicable — 20% of outperformance over benchmark, common in alternatives)
- Insurance premium (separate from administration)
Total cost of product (investment fee + administration fee + transaction costs) is the comparable metric.

### Contribution gap analysis
Gap = Target_retirement_balance - Projected_balance_at_retirement
Sensitivity levers: contribution rate increase, salary sacrifice, retirement age extension, return assumptions.

## Regulatory change watchlist (as of 2025)

- **Payday super (from 1 Jul 2026):** SG must be paid on same day as salary (not quarterly). Major operational and cashflow change for employers. Compliance risk for small employers.
- **Super on parental leave pay (from 1 Jul 2025):** Government-funded Parental Leave Pay will attract SG. Addresses systemic gender gap in super.
- **APRA Performance Test expansion:** Being extended beyond MySuper to Trustee-Directed Products (TDPs). More funds at risk.
- **Objective of Superannuation (legislated 2024):** "to preserve savings to deliver income for a dignified retirement, alongside government support, in an equitable and sustainable way." Sets policy test for future changes.
- **Franking credit debate:** Ongoing policy tension; Labor 2019 election policy to limit refunds did not pass; system currently unchanged.
- **Tax on balances > $3M (proposed from FY2026):** Earnings on the portion of super balances above $3M taxed at 30% (up from 15%). Affects ~80,000 Australians. At time of writing: legislation passed Senate committee.

## Gender gap in super

Australian women retire with approximately 20-25% less super than men. Key drivers:
1. Career breaks for caring (maternity, elder care) — period of no employer SG
2. Part-time/casual work — lower base salary, lower SG dollar amount
3. Lower lifetime earnings — gender pay gap (~13% WGEA median)
4. Investment option choice — women slightly more conservative on average
5. Insurance: historically same premium rates despite longer life expectancy (now adjusted)

Super on Parental Leave Pay (from 2025) addresses point 1 partially.

Analytical opportunity: cohort analysis by gender, parental leave year, contribution gap attribution.
